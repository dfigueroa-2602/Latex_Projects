\section{Introduction}

The precise control of DC motors is critical in numerous applications ranging from robotics and automation to electric vehicles and industrial machinery~\cite{BookUmans2013,Sul2011}. Traditional control methods, such as proportional-integral-derivative (PID) controllers, are widely used but often face limitations in handling complex, nonlinear dynamics and varying operational conditions. These limitations motivate the exploration of advanced control strategies, such as reinforcement learning (RL), capable of adapting autonomously to changing conditions and uncertainties~\cite{Arulkumaran2017}.

In this context, this project investigates the application of deep deterministic policy gradient (DDPG), a state-of-the-art RL technique. DDPG is particularly well-suited for continuous action spaces, making it an ideal candidate for controlling DC motors, where the control input is a continuous variable~\cite{Lillicrap2019}. The goal of this project is to develop a RL agent that learns to control the armature voltage applied to a DC motor in order to track a dynamically varying reference velocity. The agent will be trained using simulated data generated from a custom \textsc{Matlab Simulink} model of the DC motor, which captures the essential dynamics and characteristics of the system.

The subsequent sections detail the experimental approach, implementation specifics, and the comprehensive evaluation of the trained agent. The results highlight the advantages of RL in addressing practical challenges inherent in DC motor control, providing both theoretical insights and practical validation of the DDPG framework.

\section{DC Motor Model}

\subsection{Mathematical Model}

A DC motor can be modeled in terms of its electrical and mechanical components~\cite{Sul2011}. The electrical part consists of the armature circuit, which includes the armature inductance, resistance, and back electromotive force (EMF). The equations governing the electrical dynamics of the motor are given by:
\begin{equation}
    v_a(t) = L_A \dfrac{di_a(t)}{dt} + R_A i_a(t) + v_{\text{rot}}(t)
\end{equation}
where $L_A$ is the armature inductance, $R_A$ is the armature resistance, $V_a(t)$ is the armature voltage of the motor and $i_a(t)$ is the armature current. The back EMF, which is proportional to the angular velocity of the motor, can be expressed as:
\begin{equation}
    v_{\text{rot}}(t) = G_{fq}i_f(t) \omega_m(t)
\end{equation}
In this equation, $G_{fq}$ is the field-to-rotor flux linkage constant, $i_f(t)$ is the field current, which will be considered constant ($i_f(t) = I_F$), and $\omega_m(t)$ is the angular velocity of the motor.

The mechanical part of the motor is described by the torque equation:
\begin{align}
    T(t) &= T_e(t) - T_L(t)
    \intertext{where $T_e(t)$ is the electrical torque and $T_L(t)$ is the load torque. The electrical torque can be expressed as}
    T_e(t) &= G_{fq}I_F i_a(t)
    \intertext{and the load torque is modeled as a function of the motor speed $\omega_m(t)$ and a constant $B$ representing the load characteristics. Thus, we have}
    T_L(t) &= B\omega_m(t) \label{eq:LoadTorque}
    \intertext{The relationship between the torque and the angular velocity is given by the equation}
    T(t) &= J \dfrac{d\omega_m(t)}{dt} + \omega_o
\end{align}

Since the motor is assumed to start from rest, it will be assumed that $\omega_o = 0$.

\subsection{Laplace Transform of the DC Motor Model}
To analyze the DC motor model in the frequency domain, we can apply the Laplace transform to equations that were aforementioned before. The Laplace transform of the electrical equation is given by:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ElectricalLaplace}
    V_a(s) = L_A s I_a(s) + R_A I_a(s) + V_{\text{rot}}(s)
\end{equation}

For instance, the back EMF will be considered as a disturbance at the input of the motor plant, which depends on the field current and the angular velocity of the motor:
\begin{equation}
    V_{\text{rot}}(s) = G_{fq} I_F \omega_m(s)
\end{equation}

The mechanical equation can also be transformed into the Laplace domain:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:MechanicalLaplace}
    T(s) = J s \omega_m(s) + T_L(s)
\end{equation}
As was done with the back EMF, the load torque will be considered as a disturbance at the output of the motor plant, which depends on the angular velocity of the motor:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:LoadTorqueLaplace}
    T_L(s) = B \omega_m(s)
\end{equation}

Using the \Cref{eq:ElectricalLaplace}, the transfer function of the electrical side of the DC motor can be obtained. The transfer function from the armature voltage to the armature current can be expressed as:
\begin{equation}
    \dfrac{I_a(s)}{V_a(s)} = \dfrac{1/R_A}{\tau s + 1}
\end{equation}
where $\tau = L_A/R_A$ is the time constant of the motor.

In the same way, using the expression from \Cref{eq:LoadTorque}, and transforming it into the Laplace domain, the transfer function (or simply a gain) from the armature current to the electrical torque can be expressed as:
\begin{equation}
    \dfrac{T_e(s)}{I_a(s)} = G_{fq} I_F
\end{equation}

From the \Cref{eq:MechanicalLaplace}, the transfer function from the electrical torque to the angular velocity of the motor can be expressed as:
\begin{equation}
    \dfrac{\omega_m(s)}{T_e(s)} = \dfrac{1}{J s}
\end{equation}

\section{Data Preparation}
The dataset used for training the reinforcement learning controller was generated from a custom \textsc{Matlab Simulink} model of the DC motor. To provide variability for generalization, the simulation included randomized initial conditions for the integrator block and reference velocities.

\subsection{Randomization and Initialization}

A custom reset function, \texttt{localResetFcn}, was used to set:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Reference velocity $\eta_{\text{ref}} \in [0, 1600]$ RPM
    \item Initial motor speed $\eta_m(0)$ within $[0.5, 1.5] \cdot \omega_{\text{ref}}$
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Signal Logging and Sampling}

\begin{figure}[ht!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width = 0.7\textwidth]{Images/Sampling.pdf}
    \caption{Example of sampling of a signal $f(t)$. The red line is the original signal, and the blue line is the sampled signal $f[k]$.}

The interaction between the controller and a plant is typically discrete in digital control systems. This process involves the acquisition of signals through analog-to-digital converters (ADC), where they are triggered each sampling time, as shown in the \Cref{fig:Sampling}. After processing the signals and applying the defined control low, the controller applies the actions each sample time.

To emulate this real implementation, the DC motor was simulated with a discrete time step of $T_s = 1\ \text{ms}$, corresponding to a sampling frequency of $f_s = 1\ \text{kHz}$. This means the controller updates its actions every millisecond. Indeed, this discrete time step governs the resolution at which the agent interacts with the environment and updates its policy.

Therefore, $\omega_m[k]$ is the motor speed at time step $k$, and $V_a[k]$ is the armature voltage applied at that step.

\subsection{Preprocessing}

In control systems, it is common to use the integral of the error as part of the state representation. Therefore, the cumulative error was computed as:
\begin{equation}
    I_e[k] = \min\left( \max\left( e(k - T_s) + T_s \cdot e(k),\ -V_{\text{max}} \right),\ V_{\text{max}} \right)
\end{equation}

This integral term is bounded to prevent excessive accumulation, where $V_{\text{max}}$ is the maximum voltage applied to the motor.
Using the integral of the error, the state vector $S(t)$ at each time step was defined as:

\begin{equation}
    S[k] = 
    \begin{bmatrix}
        \omega_{\text{ref}}[k] \\
        \omega_m[k] \\
        I_e[k]
    \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

The action of the agent ($A[k]$) is the armature voltage applied at time step $k$, which is a continuous scalar value in the range $[0, 220]$ V.

\section{Training and Evaluation}

\begin{figure}[ht!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width = 0.8\textwidth]{Images/ReinforcementLearning.pdf}
    \caption{Reinforcement learning diagram}
    \label{fig:ReinforcementLearning}

The control problem was formulated as a reinforcement learning task, where the agent learns to control the DC motor by interacting with the environment. The agent receives observations from the environment, takes actions based on its policy, and receives rewards based on its performance, as is shown in \Cref{fig:ReinforcementLearning}.
The training process involved the following steps:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The agent observes the current state of the environment, represented by the state vector $S[k]$.
    \item Based on its policy, the agent selects an action $A[k]$. In this case, the action will govern the armature voltage $V_a[k]$ applied to the motor.
    \item The environment responds by updating the motor speed $\omega_m[k]$ and providing a reward $R[k]$ based on the tracking error.
    \item The agent updates its policy based on the received reward and the observed state transition.
    \item This process is repeated for a predefined number of episodes, allowing the agent to learn an optimal policy for controlling the motor.
\end{itemize}

The training was performed using the deep deterministic policy gradient (DDPG) algorithm with an actor-critic architecture. The environment was defined via \texttt{rlSimulinkEnv}, and reset logic was introduced through \texttt{localResetFcn}.

To encourage sufficient exploration of the action space, the action output of the policy was perturbed using Ornstein–Uhlenbeck noise. This allowed the agent to discover control strategies that generalize across different initial conditions and reference profiles.

\subsection{Reward Function}

The scalar reward function at each time step penalized deviation from the reference:

\begin{equation}
    R[k] = -|\omega_{\text{ref}}(k) - \omega_m(k)|
\end{equation}

\subsection{Reinforcement Learning as a Markov Decision Process}

This closed-loop structure corresponds to a Markov Decision Process (MDP), where the state includes both reference and current speed, and the reward function guides the agent toward minimal steady-state and transient error.

The action space $\mathcal{A}$ is the set of possible armature voltages $V_a[k]$, treated as a continuous scalar. The agent learns a deterministic policy $\pi: \mathcal{S} \to \mathcal{A}$ to minimize tracking error. The transition model is not known a priori but is embedded in the system dynamics simulated in \textsc{Simulink}.

The reward function $R[k]$ penalizes deviations from the desired state. The Markov property is assumed to hold: the next state depends only on the current state and action, not on prior history.

This abstraction aligns with the agent design framework discussed in the course, where intelligent decision-making under uncertainty is guided by a value function and updated via observed rewards.

\subsection{Learning Agent Architecture}

The implemented controller corresponds to a utility-based learning agent~\cite{Lillicrap2019}. This architecture decomposes into four functional components:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Performance element:} a deep neural network (DNN) policy (actor) outputs the control action, and a critic network evaluates its expected value.
    \item \textbf{Learning element:} the policy and value function are updated through \linebreak temporal-difference learning with mini-batch gradient descent, using past experience stored in a replay buffer.
    \item \textbf{Critic:} provides feedback to the actor based on the Bellman error.
    \item \textbf{Problem generator:} exploration is introduced via noise (Ornstein–Uhlenbeck process~\cite{Uhlenbeck1930}) to encourage policy improvement under unseen conditions.
\end{itemize}

This structure matches the "learning agent" model from the course: an autonomous agent perceiving a dynamic environment, adapting based on feedback, and improving performance over time. The agent is not hard-coded with control laws but discovers them through interaction, generalizing across randomized references and initial states.

The actor network takes the state vector $S[k]$ as input and passes it through three fully connected hidden layers, each containing 32 neurons with ReLU activation functions, as it shown in the \Cref{fig:NeuronalNetworkDiagram}.a). The output layer provides a single scalar action representing the armature voltage command applied to the DC motor. In the other hand, the critic network takes both the state vector $S[k]$ and the action $A[k]$ as inputs. It concatenates these inputs and passes them through three hidden layers with 64 neurons each, also using ReLU activation functions, as it shown in the \Cref{fig:NeuronalNetworkDiagram}.b). The output layer produces a scalar Q-value representing the expected return for the given state-action pair.

This can be summarized as follows:

\noindent\textbf{Actor network:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Three fully connected layers with 32 ReLU neurons each
    \item Output layer: scalar action (voltage command)
\end{itemize}

\noindent\textbf{Critic network:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Dual input (state and action) structure
    \item Three hidden layers of 64 ReLU neurons after concatenation
    \item Output layer: scalar Q-value
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Hyperparameters}

The performance and stability of deep reinforcement learning algorithms depend heavily on the choice of hyperparameters. These parameters influence the learning dynamics, balance exploration with exploitation, and affect the quality and speed of convergence to an optimal policy. In this project, the hyperparameters were carefully selected based on empirical trials and established best practices from the literature to ensure robust and efficient training of both actor and critic networks. The specific hyperparameters used for training the reinforcement learning controller are listed below:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Discount factor: $\gamma = 0.8$
    \item Mini-batch size: 512
    \item Replay buffer size: $10^6$ transitions
    \item Learning rates: $3 \cdot 10^{-4}$ (actor), $2 \cdot 10^{-4}$ (critic)
    \item Noise: Ornstein-Uhlenbeck with $\sigma = 0.3$ decaying per episode
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Training Configuration and Process}

The training process for the DDPG agent involved a comprehensive setup to ensure effective learning and stable convergence. Training was conducted over 3000 episodes, each comprising up to 3000 time steps, allowing the agent extensive interactions with the simulation environment to develop a robust policy. To systematically track and evaluate learning progress, the agent's state was automatically saved every 100 episodes via a custom callback function, facilitating analysis of intermediate performance and enabling model selection based on performance metrics.

To enhance computational efficiency and accelerate the learning process, parallel training was employed, significantly reducing the overall training duration. \Cref{fig:TrainingProcess} depicts the evolution of the reward signal across training episodes, showing both individual episode rewards and their moving average. The smooth and steadily rising trend of average rewards demonstrates successful policy optimization and highlights the algorithm's effectiveness in progressively minimizing tracking errors through iterative interactions with the environment.

\section{Results}

The performance of the trained DDPG agent was thoroughly evaluated by analyzing its capability to follow a dynamic reference angular velocity profile. Specifically, the reference angular velocity was composed of step functions changing instantaneously at $t = 0\ s$, $t = 1\ s$, and $t = 2\ s$.

\subsection{Tracking Performance}

The DDPG agent demonstrated robust tracking behavior, accurately and swiftly adjusting its control output to follow the reference angular velocity, as illustrated in \Cref{fig:MotorSpeed}. The observed motor speed closely matched the reference profile, exhibiting minimal overshoot at transition instants and achieving low steady-state errors. This indicates the agent successfully generalized its learned policy to manage rapid setpoint changes, showcasing strong transient performance and consistent steady-state accuracy.

Additionally, despite the randomized initial conditions, the agent consistently stabilized the motor's speed, reflecting effective training and adequate exploration during the learning phase. These results highlight the strength of reinforcement learning in handling nonlinear dynamics and uncertainties inherent to DC motor systems.

\begin{equation}

\end{equation}

\section{Conclusion}

In this report, it was demonstrated that the DDPG algorithm is an effective and robust method for controlling DC motors in dynamic and uncertain conditions. The trained agent consistently exhibited accurate tracking performance with minimal overshoot and low \linebreak steady-state error, even under varying reference velocities and randomized initial conditions. Furthermore, it was observed that the applied control voltages were smooth and consistently bounded within practical operational limits, ensuring mechanical stability and reliability.

The reward curve clearly illustrated progressive improvement, confirming that the agent successfully learned an optimal control policy through RL. This smooth convergence pattern underscored the stability and effectiveness of the chosen training methodology and hyperparameter settings.

Overall, the findings from this project provide strong support for the adoption of RL techniques in practical control scenarios, highlighting their advantages over traditional methods in terms of adaptability, robustness, and overall performance.
