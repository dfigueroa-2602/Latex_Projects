\section{Introduction}

In power electronics, the usage of DC motors is prevalent due to their simplicity and efficiency~\cite{BookUmans2013}. These motor can be found in various applications, from small toys to large industrial machines. The control of these motors is crucial for their performance and efficiency.

Generally, the DC motors are attached to a load, which can be anything from a simple fan to a complex robotic arm. The load characteristics can significantly affect the performance of the motor, and thus, it is essential to model the motor and its load accurately. An example of a DC motor and its load is shown in the \Cref{fig:DCMotor}.

In this report, we will explore the application of machine learning techniques to model and control a DC motor.

\section{DC Motor Model}

\subsection{Mathematical Model}

A DC motor can be modeled in terms of its electrical and mechanical components. The electrical part consists of the armature circuit, which includes the armature inductance, resistance, and back electromotive force (EMF). The equations governing the electrical dynamics of the motor are given by:
\begin{equation}
    V_a(t) = L_A \dfrac{di_a(t)}{dt} + R_A i_a(t) + V_{\text{rot}}(t)
\end{equation}
where $L_A$ is the armature inductance, $R_A$ is the armature resistance, $V_a(t)$ is the armature voltage of the motor and $i_f(t)$ is the field current. For simplicity, in this work the field current will be considered constant ($i_f(t) = I_F$). $V_{\text{rot}}(t)$ is the back EMF given by:
\begin{equation}
    V_{\text{rot}}(t) = G_{fq}i_f(t) \omega_m(t)
\end{equation}
In this equation, $G_{fq}$ is the field-to-rotor flux linkage constant and $\omega_m(t)$ is the angular velocity of the motor.
The mechanical part of the motor is described by the torque equation:
\begin{align}
    T(t) &= T_e(t) - T_L(t)
    \intertext{where $T_e(t)$ is the electrical torque and $T_L(t)$ is the load torque. The electrical torque can be expressed as}
    T_e(t) &= G_{fq}I_F i_a(t)
    \intertext{and the load torque is modeled as a function of the motor speed $\omega_m(t)$ and a constant $B$ representing the load characteristics. Thus, we have}
    T_L(t) &= B\omega_m(t) \label{eq:LoadTorque}
    \intertext{The relationship between the torque and the angular velocity is given by the equation}
    T(t) &= J \dfrac{d\omega_m(t)}{dt} + \omega_o
\end{align}

Since the motor is assumed to start from rest, it will be assumed that $\omega_o = 0$.

\subsection{Laplace Transform of the DC Motor Model}
To analyze the DC motor model in the frequency domain, we can apply the Laplace transform to the equations. The Laplace transform of the electrical equation is given by:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ElectricalLaplace}
    V_a(s) = L_A s I_a(s) + R_A I_a(s) + V_{\text{rot}}(s)
\end{equation}

For instance, the back EMF will be considered as a disturbance at the input of the motor plant, which depends on the field current and the angular velocity of the motor:
\begin{equation}
    V_{\text{rot}}(s) = G_{fq} I_F \omega_m(s)
\end{equation}

The mechanical equation can also be transformed into the Laplace domain:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:MechanicalLaplace}
    T(s) = J s \omega_m(s) + T_L(s)
\end{equation}
As was done with the back EMF, the load torque will be considered as a disturbance at the output of the motor plant, which depends on the angular velocity of the motor:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:LoadTorqueLaplace}
    T_L(s) = B \omega_m(s)
\end{equation}

Using the \Cref{eq:ElectricalLaplace}, the transfer function of the electrical side of the DC motor can be obtained. The transfer function from the armature voltage to the armature current can be expressed as:
\begin{equation}
    \dfrac{I_a(s)}{V_a(s)} = \dfrac{1/R_A}{\tau s + 1}
\end{equation}
where $\tau = L_A/R_A$ is the time constant of the motor.

In the same way, using the expression from \Cref{eq:LoadTorque}, and transforming it into the Laplace domain, the transfer function (or simply a gain) from the armature current to the electrical torque can be expressed as:
\begin{equation}
    \dfrac{T_e(s)}{I_a(s)} = G_{fq} I_F
\end{equation}

From the \Cref{eq:MechanicalLaplace}, the transfer function from the electrical torque to the angular velocity of the motor can be expressed as:
\begin{equation}
    \dfrac{\omega_m(s)}{T_e(s)} = \dfrac{1}{J s}
\end{equation}

The block diagram of the DC motor model can be represented as shown in \Cref{fig:DCMotorModel}. The input to the system is the armature voltage $V_a(s)$, and the output is the angular velocity of the motor $\omega_m(s)$.

\section{Data Preparation}
The dataset used for training the reinforcement learning controller was generated from a custom \textsc{Matlab Simulink} model of the DC motor. To provide variability for generalization, the simulation included randomized initial conditions for the integrator block and reference velocities.

\subsection{Randomization and Initialization}

A custom reset function, \texttt{localResetFcn}, was used to set:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Reference velocity $\omega_{\text{ref}} \in [0, 1600]$ RPM
    \item Initial motor speed $\omega_m(0)$ within $[0.5, 1.5] \cdot \omega_{\text{ref}}$
\end{itemize}

This stochastic reset ensures coverage of a wide operating space, enabling robust policy learning.

\subsection{Signal Logging and Sampling}

Each episode lasted $T_f = 3$ seconds with a time step $T_s = 1$ ms. The simulation logged:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Armature voltage $V_a(t)$
    \item Measured speed $\omega_m(t)$
    \item Reference speed $\omega_{\text{ref}}(t)$
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Preprocessing}

All signals were measured in SI units. Angular velocity of the motor is expressed in rad/s and voltage expressed in V. The observation vector was:
\[
\mathbf{o}(t) = 
\begin{bmatrix}
\omega_{\text{ref}}(t) \\
\omega_m(t) \\
\omega_{\text{ref}}(t) - \omega_m(t)
\end{bmatrix}
\]


\section{Training and Evaluation}

The training was performed using the deep deterministic policy gradient (DDPG) algorithm with an actor-critic architecture. The environment was defined via \texttt{rlSimulinkEnv}, and reset logic was introduced through \texttt{localResetFcn}.

\subsection{Reward Function}

The scalar reward function at each time step penalized deviation from the reference:
\[
r(t) = -|\omega_{\text{ref}}(t) - \omega_m(t)|
\]

\subsection{Reinforcement Learning as a Markov Decision Process}

The control problem was formulated as a Markov Decision Process (MDP), a foundational concept in reinforcement learning. The state space $\mathcal{S}$ consists of a three-dimensional vector:
\[
s_t = 
\begin{bmatrix}
\omega_{\text{ref}}(t) \\
\omega_m(t) \\
\omega_{\text{ref}}(t) - \omega_m(t)
\end{bmatrix},
\]
representing the reference velocity, the current motor speed, and the tracking error.

The action space $\mathcal{A}$ is the set of possible armature voltages $V_a(t)$, treated as a continuous scalar. The agent learns a deterministic policy $\pi: \mathcal{S} \to \mathcal{A}$ to minimize tracking error. The transition model is not known a priori but is embedded in the system dynamics simulated in \textsc{Simulink}.

The reward function $r(s_t, a_t)$ penalizes deviations from the desired state. The Markov property is assumed to hold: the next state depends only on the current state and action, not on prior history.

This abstraction aligns with the agent design framework discussed in the course, where intelligent decision-making under uncertainty is guided by a value function and updated via observed rewards.

\subsection{Learning Agent Architecture}

The implemented controller corresponds to a utility-based learning agent as introduced in the course. This architecture decomposes into four functional components:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Performance element:} a deep neural network policy (actor) outputs the control action, and a critic network evaluates its expected value.
    \item \textbf{Learning element:} the policy and value function are updated through temporal-difference learning with mini-batch gradient descent, using past experience stored in a replay buffer.
    \item \textbf{Critic:} provides feedback to the actor based on the Bellman error.
    \item \textbf{Problem generator:} exploration is introduced via noise (Ornstein–Uhlenbeck process) to encourage policy improvement under unseen conditions.
\end{itemize}

This structure matches the "learning agent" model from the course: an autonomous agent perceiving a dynamic environment, adapting based on feedback, and improving performance over time. The agent is not hard-coded with control laws but discovers them through interaction, generalizing across randomized references and initial states.

\subsection{Network Architectures}

\textbf{Actor network:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Three fully connected layers with 32 ReLU neurons each
    \item Output layer: scalar action (voltage command)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Critic network:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Dual input (state and action) structure
    \item Three hidden layers of 64 ReLU neurons after concatenation
    \item Output layer: scalar Q-value
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Hyperparameters}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Discount factor: $\gamma = 0.8$
    \item Mini-batch size: 512
    \item Replay buffer size: $10^6$ transitions
    \item Learning rates: $3 \cdot 10^{-4}$ (actor), $2 \cdot 10^{-4}$ (critic)
    \item Noise: Ornstein-Uhlenbeck with $\sigma = 0.3$ decaying per episode
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Training Configuration}

Training ran for 3000 episodes with up to 3000 steps each. Agents were auto-saved every 100 episodes using a custom callback. Parallel training was enabled to speed up convergence.


\section{Results}

The performance of the trained DDPG agent was evaluated on previously unseen reference profiles, including step and ramp changes in desired angular velocity. The following observations were made:

\subsection{Tracking Performance}

The agent consistently tracked the reference angular velocity with minimal overshoot and low steady-state error. Even with randomized starting speeds and varying targets, the agent achieved stable responses.

\subsection{Control Behavior}

The applied control voltage $V_a(t)$ was smooth and bounded within the physical range of $[0, 220]$ V, exhibiting gradual changes even in response to steep velocity transitions.

\subsection{Comparison to Classical Control}

A comparison with a tuned PI controller demonstrated that the RL controller had:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Faster settling time
    \item Lower steady-state error
    \item Smoother control action
\end{itemize}
